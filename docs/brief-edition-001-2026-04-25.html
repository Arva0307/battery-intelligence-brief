FEOC Compliance Deadline Arrives — 55% Non-FEOC Cost Threshold Now a Hard Gate on ITC Eligibility

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act's Foreign Entity of Concern restrictions took full effect in 2026, requiring at least 55% of project costs to originate from non-FEOC sources for ITC eligibility — a threshold rising to 75% by 2030. For developers with projects not safe-harbored before January 1, 2026, compliance is now a hard gate on tax credit access. Treasury's interim guidance (Notice 2026-15) established a Material Assistance Cost Ratio framework but left critical interpretive questions unanswered, particularly for public companies attempting to verify equity and debt ownership thresholds against the prohibited foreign entity definition.

The supply constraint is real: Korean manufacturers AESC (Tennessee) and LG Energy Solution (Michigan) account for over 80% of available FEOC-compliant ESS cell capacity, but those facilities are still ramping from EV battery retooling. If plants operate at the industry-historical 70–80% utilization, the apparent 10% domestic capacity surplus evaporates quickly. Fluence is already using AESC cells for a portion of U.S. projects. Developers initiating new projects without secured FEOC-compliant supply face cost increases exceeding 30% depending on applicable tariff levels.

Synthesis — Test Engineering & Validation Lens

FEOC compliance is now a procurement-stage validation requirement. Labs and certification teams will increasingly be called upon to verify bill-of-materials traceability at the cell level — a workflow that touches UL 9540 listing, supply chain documentation, and AHJ review packages. Test engineers who can speak fluently to cell-origin provenance in a certification dossier have a distinct advantage in 2026 project conversations.

SafetyStorage

UL 9540A 6th Edition Published March 13 — Large-Scale Fire Testing Is Now the Certification Baseline

UL 9540A Edition 6 was officially published March 13, 2026, formalizing large-scale fire testing (LSFT) as the new standard for installation-level BESS certification. The update aligns directly with NFPA 855 (2026 edition), which mandates in Section 9.2.1.2.1 that BESS installations demonstrate non-propagation between adjacent units under real fire conditions. The 6th Edition introduces a post-deflagration condition to evaluate enclosure design, separation distances, and fire suppression effectiveness — a significant step beyond the module-level optimization that defined prior generations of BESS safety design.

Component-level compliance is no longer sufficient. Manufacturers must now demonstrate full system behavior under fire, including intentional ignition of vented gases. AHJs are increasingly requesting heat release rate (HRR) data, gas composition analysis, and BMS response logs that go beyond what UL 9540A alone requires. CSA/ANSI C800:25 is emerging as a complementary standard providing the performance-based engineering data that fire protection engineers need for site-specific AHJ approvals. The practical effect: certification now requires planning, testing, and documentation at a scale most BESS programs haven't encountered before.

Synthesis — Certification Strategy Lens

Ed. 5 test reports are no longer sufficient for new project approvals under 2026 NFPA 855. Programs in development today must budget for LSFT at the installation level, requiring dedicated lab capacity with calorimetry infrastructure. Manufacturers who proactively run Ed. 6 tests and build comprehensive LSFT data packages will have a meaningful lead-time advantage over competitors still relying on legacy reports.

MarketStorage

"Unfettered Optimism" on BESS Degradation Meets Operational Reality at US Storage Summit 2026

Industry leaders at the 2026 US Energy Storage Summit in Dallas issued a direct warning: typical grid-scale BESS assets are projected to lose 20–30% of capacity in the first decade, and merchant-cycled systems are on pace to experience degradation costing tens of millions of dollars in project valuation. FlexGen's Director of Data Analytics identified cell imbalance — not calendar aging — as the primary near-term bottleneck, with passive BMS balancing approaches burning off excess energy via resistors rather than recovering it. Active balancing, which redistributes current to equalize cell voltage, is the preferred mitigation but requires more sophisticated BMS integration not standard in most deployed systems.

Bank of America's Director of GRE Technical flagged auxiliary loads as a persistent blind spot in project financial models, noting that extreme ambient conditions (107°F in Arizona) drive thermal management energy draw far above modeled assumptions. The combined effect of cell imbalance and auxiliary load underestimation is systematic overstatement of available revenue. Capacity test methodologies during commissioning were called out as a critical quality gate — catching "bad cells" early allows warranty claims before long-term performance contracts are locked in.

Synthesis — Validation & Test Engineering Lens

The degradation data gap is a direct validation problem. Commissioning-phase performance testing protocols — including deliberate early cycling, cell imbalance characterization, and auxiliary load baselining under thermal extremes — are becoming differentiating capabilities for test engineers. Programs that build rigorous site acceptance test sequences and capacity verification protocols are directly protecting long-term project value.

Prioritize relationship-building in Virginia, California, and New York state procurement ecosystems where mandate pipelines are deepest.

Storage retains robust ITC/PTC support through 2033 under the OBBBA, but supply chain restrictions have introduced a cost premium of $10–30/kWh for fully FEOC-compliant systems. While project developers received ITC FEOC safe harbour guidance via Notice 2026-15, equivalent guidance for 45X manufacturing credit claimants has not been published. BNEF expects IRS to publish by end of 2027 — meaning domestic manufacturers are operating without definitive compliance guidance for up to 18 more months, creating real risk of credit recapture for facilities making sourcing decisions today based on assumptions that may later be corrected.

Systematic underestimation of auxiliary load consumption — particularly thermal management energy draw under extreme ambient conditions — is creating a persistent gap between modeled and operational BESS performance. Projects in hot-climate regions (Arizona, Texas, Southeast) are most exposed. Financiers are beginning to require more conservative auxiliary load assumptions and independent commissioning validation, which could affect financing terms for projects with legacy financial models.

👁

Industry observers describe non-flammable chemistry adoption in wildfire-prone regions as a matter of "when, not if" following a trigger incident. The Moss Landing fire established the regulatory template; insurers are already pricing chemistry risk differentially. A second high-profile incident before 2027 could catalyze AHJ-driven chemistry restrictions reshaping procurement specs for an estimated 15+ GW of California storage pipeline in ways not captured in current financial models.

🌱 Research Translation
Omri Tayyara, Ph.D. · IndustryWeek / CSA Group · April 2026 · "What the 6th Edition of UL 9540A Means for Battery Storage Safety Certification"
The Certification Floor Moved — And Most BESS Programs Haven't Caught Up Yet
What the Research Says

The 6th Edition of UL 9540A formalizes large-scale fire testing (LSFT) as the certification baseline for BESS installations. But the critical insight from Tayyara's analysis is that UL 9540A Ed. 6 is a necessary but not sufficient standard for real project approval — it establishes the certification floor, not the ceiling. AHJs and fire protection engineers consistently require heat release rate (HRR) data, gas composition analysis, and BMS response data that are explicitly outside UL 9540A's scope. CSA/ANSI C800:25 exists precisely to fill this gap, and the two standards together now form the practical certification package for complex or novel installations. NFPA 855-2026 §4.4.2.3 adds a further layer: the HMA must evaluate the reliability and survivability of critical safety components during thermal runaway propagation — not just demonstrate non-propagation in a lab configuration.

Why This Matters Right Now

Programs being designed today for 2026–2027 construction starts are planning test programs against Ed. 5 norms. The design, test, and documentation effort required to satisfy Ed. 6 + NFPA 855 (2026) + CSA C800:25 is meaningfully larger — likely adding 8–16 weeks to a typical certification timeline and potentially $500K–$1.5M in additional test costs for full LSFT on large container systems. Programs that don't budget for this now will face costly re-work at the AHJ review stage, often after engineering is already locked.

Original Intelligence — Not in the Paper

The Cell-Change Protocol Gap: The intersection of FEOC compliance and certification testing creates an underappreciated double-exposure for manufacturers. A program that runs a full Ed. 6 LSFT with a Chinese-cell configuration — and subsequently needs to switch to FEOC-compliant Korean cells — must evaluate whether the test data remains valid for the new cell variant. Cell-level performance characteristics (thermal runaway onset temperature, gas composition, HRR) can differ meaningfully between cell manufacturers. This could force repeat LSFT or at minimum require a change-in-design engineering analysis — neither reflected in current certification program budgets for 2026 product launches. No standard protocol yet exists to govern this scenario.

🟢 Confidence Level: High — Multiple independent sources confirm; standard text publicly available; CSA Group, UL Solutions, and Intertek commentary corroborates interpretation
Implementation Path
  • Audit all active BESS certification programs for UL 9540A edition version — any program relying solely on Ed. 5 installation-level reports must be flagged for AHJ approval risk under 2026 NFPA 855 adoptions
  • Build CSA/ANSI C800:25 test scope into standard LSFT quotations proactively — offer HRR, gas composition, and detection response as a bundled package rather than responding to AHJ RFIs after the test window closes
  • Develop a "cell change protocol" document outlining the minimum additional testing required when a FEOC-driven cell substitution occurs after initial UL 9540A LSFT completion — this is a value-add service the market needs urgently in 2026
  • Engage manufacturers on HMA planning early in product design: position test labs as partners in the HMA process, not just test executors, to capture higher-value project involvement and reduce permitting surprises
  • For second-life EV battery programs, begin developing a UL 9540A LSFT test plan template specific to repurposed-pack configurations — no standard exists yet and the first lab to publish methodology guidance captures disproportionate early project flow
📌 Steel-Manned Counterargument

The certification complexity narrative may overstate the practical barrier. Many AHJs, particularly in states with limited ESS experience, continue to accept Ed. 5 documentation and rely on prescriptive NFPA 855 spacing tables rather than performance-based LSFT outcomes. For straightforward outdoor containerized utility-scale projects in non-wildfire-prone jurisdictions, the marginal cost of upgrading from Ed. 5 to Ed. 6 compliance may not be justified by approval timeline benefits — especially if Ed. 6 adoption by state and local codes lags the NFPA publication cycle by the historically typical 2–3 years.
